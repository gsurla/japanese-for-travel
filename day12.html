Shopping Vocabulary (15 minutes)

Shopping is an essential part of traveling in Japan, whether you're buying souvenirs, necessities, or just browsing. Let's learn key shopping-related vocabulary:

Basic Shopping Words:

お店 (omise) - shop/store

Pronunciation: o-mi-se

Example: このお店は何時に開きますか？ (Kono omise wa nanji ni akimasu ka?) - What time does this store open?

Cultural note: Many shops in tourist areas have tax-free options for foreign visitors

買い物 (kaimono) - shopping

Pronunciation: ka-i-mo-no

Example: 買い物に行きます (Kaimono ni ikimasu) - I'm going shopping

Cultural note: Shopping is a popular activity in Japan, with many unique items not available elsewhere

値段 (nedan) - price

Pronunciation: ne-dan

Example: この値段はいくらですか？ (Kono nedan wa ikura desu ka?) - How much is this price?

Cultural note: Bargaining is not common in Japan; prices are generally fixed

安い (yasui) - cheap/inexpensive

Pronunciation: ya-su-i

Example: これは安いです (Kore wa yasui desu) - This is inexpensive

Cultural note: The word can also mean "easy" in different contexts

高い (takai) - expensive/high

Pronunciation: ta-ka-i

Example: ちょっと高いです (Chotto takai desu) - It's a bit expensive

Cultural note: The word can also mean "tall" in different contexts

サイズ (saizu) - size

Pronunciation: sa-i-zu

Example: このサイズはありますか？ (Kono saizu wa arimasu ka?) - Do you have this size?

Cultural note: Japanese clothing sizes tend to run smaller than Western sizes

色 (iro) - color

Pronunciation: i-ro

Example: 他の色はありますか？ (Hoka no iro wa arimasu ka?) - Do you have other colors?

Cultural note: Traditional Japanese colors have specific names and cultural significance

Types of Shops:

  • デパート (depaato) - department store
  • スーパー (suupaa) - supermarket
  • コンビニ (konbini) - convenience store
  • ショッピングモール (shoppingu mooru) - shopping mall
  • 市場 (ichiba) - market
  • 薬局 (yakkyoku) - pharmacy
  • 本屋 (honya) - bookstore
  • お土産屋 (omiyageya) - souvenir shop
  • 電気屋 (denki-ya) - electronics store
  • 洋服屋 (youfuku-ya) - clothing store

Shopping Items:

  • 服 (fuku) - clothes
  • 靴 (kutsu) - shoes
  • 帽子 (boushi) - hat
  • かばん (kaban) - bag
  • 財布 (saifu) - wallet
  • 時計 (tokei) - watch/clock
  • お土産 (omiyage) - souvenir
  • 食べ物 (tabemono) - food
  • 飲み物 (nomimono) - drink
  • 薬 (kusuri) - medicine

Payment Terms:

  • お金 (okane) - money
  • 現金 (genkin) - cash
  • クレジットカード (kurejitto kaado) - credit card
  • レシート (reshiito) - receipt
  • 消費税 (shouhizei) - consumption tax
  • 免税 (menzei) - tax-free
  • 割引 (waribiki) - discount
  • お釣り (otsuri) - change (money)

Essential Shopping Phrases (15 minutes)

Let's learn key phrases for shopping in Japan:

1. これはいくらですか？ (Kore wa ikura desu ka?) - How much is this?

Pronunciation: ko-re wa i-ku-ra des-ka

Usage: The most basic way to ask for the price of an item

Structure:

  • これ (kore) - this
  • は (wa) - topic marker
  • いくら (ikura) - how much
  • ですか (desu ka) - is?

Example dialogue:

A: これはいくらですか？ (Kore wa ikura desu ka?)
How much is this?

B: 1,500円です。 (Sen-gohyaku-en desu.)
It's 1,500 yen.

A: ありがとうございます。 (Arigatou gozaimasu.)
Thank you.

Variations:

- それはいくらですか？ (Sore wa ikura desu ka?)
How much is that? (item near listener)

- あれはいくらですか？ (Are wa ikura desu ka?)
How much is that? (item far from both)

- 全部でいくらですか？ (Zenbu de ikura desu ka?)
How much is it in total?

2. これをください。 (Kore wo kudasai.) - I'll take this, please.

Pronunciation: ko-re wo ku-da-sa-i

Usage: Use this phrase when you've decided to purchase an item

Structure:

  • これ (kore) - this
  • を (wo) - object marker
  • ください (kudasai) - please give me

Example dialogue:

A: これをください。 (Kore wo kudasai.)
I'll take this, please.

B: はい、1,200円です。 (Hai, sen-nihyaku-en desu.)
Yes, that's 1,200 yen.

A: はい、どうぞ。 (Hai, douzo.)
Here you are. (while handing money)

Variations:

- これを2つください。 (Kore wo futatsu kudasai.)
I'll take two of these, please.

- 赤いのをください。 (Akai no wo kudasai.)
I'll take the red one, please.

- あれもください。 (Are mo kudasai.)
I'll take that one too, please.

3. 試着できますか？ (Shichaku dekimasu ka?) - Can I try it on?

Pronunciation: shi-cha-ku de-ki-ma-su ka

Usage: Use this phrase when you want to try on clothing

Structure:

  • 試着 (shichaku) - trying on clothes
  • できます (dekimasu) - can do
  • か (ka) - question marker

Example dialogue:

A: この服を試着できますか？ (Kono fuku wo shichaku dekimasu ka?)
Can I try on these clothes?

B: はい、試着室はあちらです。 (Hai, shichakushitsu wa achira desu.)
Yes, the fitting room is over there.

A: ありがとうございます。 (Arigatou gozaimasu.)
Thank you.

Variations:

- 試着室はどこですか？ (Shichakushitsu wa doko desu ka?)
Where is the fitting room?

- これは試着できますか？ (Kore wa shichaku dekimasu ka?)
Can I try this on?

4. クレジットカードは使えますか？ (Kurejitto kaado wa tsukaemasu ka?) - Can I use a credit card?

Pronunciation: ku-re-jit-to kaa-do wa tsu-ka-e-ma-su ka

Usage: Use this phrase to ask if credit cards are accepted

Structure:

  • クレジットカード (kurejitto kaado) - credit card
  • は (wa) - topic marker
  • 使えます (tsukaemasu) - can use
  • か (ka) - question marker

Example dialogue:

A: クレジットカードは使えますか？ (Kurejitto kaado wa tsukaemasu ka?)
Can I use a credit card?

B: はい、使えます。 (Hai, tsukaemasu.)
Yes, you can.

A: ありがとうございます。 (Arigatou gozaimasu.)
Thank you.

Variations:

- 現金だけですか？ (Genkin dake desu ka?)
Cash only?

- VISAカードは使えますか？ (VISA kaado wa tsukaemasu ka?)
Can I use a VISA card?

5. 免税になりますか？ (Menzei ni narimasu ka?) - Is this tax-free?

Pronunciation: men-zei ni na-ri-ma-su ka

Usage: Use this phrase to ask if tax-free shopping is available (for tourists)

Structure:

  • 免税 (menzei) - tax-free
  • に (ni) - direction particle
  • なります (narimasu) - become
  • か (ka) - question marker

Example dialogue:

A: 免税になりますか？ (Menzei ni narimasu ka?)
Is this tax-free?

B: はい、パスポートを見せてください。 (Hai, pasupooto wo misete kudasai.)
Yes, please show me your passport.

A: はい、どうぞ。 (Hai, douzo.)
Yes, here you are.

Variations:

- 免税店はどこですか？ (Menzei-ten wa doko desu ka?)
Where is the tax-free shop?

- 外国人割引はありますか？ (Gaikokujin waribiki wa arimasu ka?)
Is there a discount for foreigners?

Additional Useful Shopping Phrases:

  • 他の色はありますか？ (Hoka no iro wa arimasu ka?)
    Do you have other colors?
  • 大きいサイズはありますか？ (Ookii saizu wa arimasu ka?)
    Do you have a larger size?
  • 小さいサイズはありますか？ (Chiisai saizu wa arimasu ka?)
    Do you have a smaller size?
  • これは何ですか？ (Kore wa nan desu ka?)
    What is this?
  • どうやって使いますか？ (Douyatte tsukaimasu ka?)
    How do you use this?
  • 包装してください。 (Housou shite kudasai.)
    Please wrap it. (for a gift)
  • 袋をください。 (Fukuro wo kudasai.)
    Please give me a bag.
  • レシートをください。 (Reshiito wo kudasai.)
    Please give me a receipt.

Tax-Free Shopping in Japan (5 minutes)

As a tourist in Japan, you can shop tax-free at many stores:

What is Tax-Free Shopping?

In Japan, there is a 10% consumption tax on most items. However, foreign visitors staying less than 6 months can shop tax-free at participating stores for purchases over 5,000 yen.

How to Shop Tax-Free:

  1. Look for the Tax-Free Sign: Stores that offer tax-free shopping display a "Tax-Free" sign.
  2. Meet the Minimum Purchase: You must spend at least 5,000 yen in a single store on the same day.
  3. Show Your Passport: You must present your passport (not a copy) at the time of purchase.
  4. Categories of Tax-Free Items:
    • General Items: Electronics, clothing, watches, bags, etc.
    • Consumable Items: Food, drinks, cosmetics, medicines, etc.

Cultural Note: Shopping in Japan

Shopping Etiquette

In Japan, customer service is taken very seriously. Shop staff will often greet you with "いらっしゃいませ" (irasshaimase - welcome) when you enter a store. When you're browsing, it's completely acceptable to look at items without purchasing, and staff will generally not pressure you to buy.

Cash vs. Credit

While major stores and shopping areas in cities accept credit cards, Japan is still largely a cash-based society. It's a good idea to carry cash, especially when shopping in smaller stores or markets. Many ATMs in convenience stores accept foreign cards.

Omiyage (Souvenir) Culture

In Japan, there's a strong culture of buying souvenirs (omiyage) for friends, family, and coworkers when traveling. These are typically regional food specialties or small items that represent the area visited. Many stations, airports, and tourist sites have dedicated omiyage shops.
