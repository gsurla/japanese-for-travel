Going to an Izakaya (Japanese Pub)

Izakayas are casual Japanese pubs where people go to drink and enjoy small dishes. This guide will help you navigate the izakaya experience with essential phrases and cultural tips.

What is an Izakaya?

An izakaya (居酒屋) is a type of informal Japanese pub that serves alcoholic drinks and small dishes for sharing. It's similar to Spanish tapas bars or American gastropubs. Izakayas are popular places for after-work drinking, socializing with friends, and casual dining.

The word "izakaya" comes from "i" (to stay) and "sakaya" (sake shop), reflecting their origin as places where people could drink sake on the premises.

Cultural Tips for Izakayas

  • Otoshi (お通し): Most izakayas serve a small appetizer called "otoshi" or "tsukidashi" when you sit down. This is not free but serves as a seating charge.
  • Sharing dishes: Food at izakayas is meant to be shared. Order several small dishes for the table rather than individual meals.
  • Ordering pace: Unlike Western restaurants, at izakayas you typically order a few dishes at a time, then order more as you go.
  • Pouring drinks: It's customary to pour drinks for others in your group rather than for yourself. Your companions will return the favor.
  • Saying "kanpai": When toasting, make eye contact with others and say "kanpai" (cheers) before drinking.
  • Using oshibori: The wet towel provided is for cleaning your hands, not your face or the table.
