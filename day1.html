Introduction to Japanese Writing Systems (5 minutes)

Japanese uses three writing systems: Hiragana, Katakana, and Kanji. Understanding these systems will help you navigate Japan more easily.

Hiragana (ひらがな)

Hiragana is a phonetic script used for native Japanese words and grammatical elements. Each character represents a syllable.

Examples: あ (a), い (i), う (u), え (e), お (o)

Katakana (カタカナ)

Katakana is used primarily for foreign words, names, and emphasis. It also represents syllables but has a more angular appearance.

Examples: ア (a), イ (i), ウ (u), エ (e), オ (o)

Kanji (漢字)

Kanji are Chinese characters adopted into Japanese. Each character can have multiple readings and meanings.

Examples: 日 (sun/day), 本 (origin/book), 人 (person), 水 (water)

For this travel course, we'll focus primarily on learning hiragana and some essential kanji and katakana words that you'll encounter during your trip.

Basic Greetings (10 minutes)

Let's learn five essential Japanese greetings that you'll use daily during your trip:

こんにちは (Konnichiwa)

Pronunciation: kon-nee-chee-wah

Meaning: Hello / Good afternoon

Usage: This is the standard greeting used during daytime (roughly from 10am to early evening).

Cultural note: A slight bow typically accompanies this greeting.

おはようございます (Ohayou gozaimasu)

Pronunciation: oh-hah-yoh goh-zah-ee-mahs

Meaning: Good morning

Usage: Used in the morning until around 10am.

Cultural note: A casual version is just "おはよう" (Ohayou), but the polite form is better for travelers.

こんばんは (Konbanwa)

Pronunciation: kon-bahn-wah

Meaning: Good evening

Usage: Used in the evening and night.

Cultural note: The "n" sound is slightly prolonged.

さようなら (Sayounara)

Pronunciation: sah-yoh-nah-rah

Meaning: Goodbye

Usage: Used when parting for a longer period. For casual goodbyes, "じゃあね" (Jaa ne) or "またね" (Mata ne) are more common.

Cultural note: This is a more formal goodbye, implying you won't see the person again for some time.

ありがとうございます (Arigatou gozaimasu)

Pronunciation: ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zah-ee-mahs

Meaning: Thank you

Usage: Used to express gratitude in most situations.

Cultural note: A slight bow often accompanies this expression. A casual version is just "ありがとう" (Arigatou).

Pronunciation Practice (10 minutes)

Japanese pronunciation is relatively straightforward, with consistent sounds. Here are some key points:

Vowel Sounds

  • a - like the "a" in "father"
  • i - like the "ee" in "feet"
  • u - like the "oo" in "boot" but with less rounded lips
  • e - like the "e" in "pet"
  • o - like the "o" in "go"

Consonant Sounds

Most consonants are similar to English, with a few exceptions:

  • r - a sound between "r" and "l"
  • g - always hard as in "go"
  • h - slightly more aspirated than in English

Rhythm and Stress

Japanese is a mora-timed language, meaning each syllable gets roughly equal stress and timing. Unlike English, there's no strong stress on particular syllables.

Practice Exercise

Repeat each greeting aloud 5 times, focusing on proper pronunciation:

  1. こんにちは (Konnichiwa) - kon-nee-chee-wah
  2. おはようございます (Ohayou gozaimasu) - oh-hah-yoh goh-zah-ee-mahs
  3. こんばんは (Konbanwa) - kon-bahn-wah
  4. さようなら (Sayounara) - sah-yoh-nah-rah
  5. ありがとうございます (Arigatou gozaimasu) - ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zah-ee-mahs

Self-Introduction (5 minutes)

Learning to introduce yourself is essential for making connections during your travels.

わたしは [name] です (Watashi wa [name] desu)

Pronunciation: wah-tah-shee wah [name] des

Meaning: I am [name]

Usage: Basic self-introduction formula.

Structure:

  • わたし (watashi) - I/me
  • は (wa) - topic marker particle
  • [name] - your name
  • です (desu) - am/is/are (copula)

はじめまして (Hajimemashite)

Pronunciation: hah-jee-meh-mah-shee-teh

Meaning: Nice to meet you (lit. "For the first time")

Usage: Used when meeting someone for the first time.

Cultural note: This is typically said before introducing yourself.

どうぞよろしく (Douzo yoroshiku)

Pronunciation: doh-zo yo-roh-shee-koo

Meaning: Please be kind to me/Please treat me well

Usage: A polite phrase that often follows self-introductions.

Cultural note: This phrase has no direct English equivalent but is an important part of Japanese introductions.

Practice Exercise

Practice a complete self-introduction:

はじめまして。わたしは [your name] です。どうぞよろしく。

Hajimemashite. Watashi wa [your name] desu. Douzo yoroshiku.

Nice to meet you. I am [your name]. Please treat me well.
